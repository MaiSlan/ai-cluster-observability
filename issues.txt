1. The Anatomy of the Simulated Faults
We injected a cascading failure. In distributed systems, a failure at the bottom layer (hardware) bubbles up to destroy the top layer (user experience).

Hardware (The Physical Layer):

The Fake Issue: Erratic utilization and massive power spikes pushing temperatures to 95°C.

The Real-World Meaning: A cooling fan died, or a liquid cooling pump failed. Silicon has physical limits. When a GPU hits 90°C+, it triggers Thermal Throttling—it intentionally slows down its own clock speed to prevent melting.

NCCL / Network (The Communication Layer):

The Fake Issue: Bandwidth dropping from 400Gbps to 20Gbps, triggering the straggler_detected flag.

The Real-World Meaning: AI training relies on synchronous math. If 8 GPUs are training a model, they all must share their results before moving to the next step. If a physical InfiniBand cable is damaged, or a network switch is misconfigured, one GPU takes 10x longer to share its data. That one "straggler" forces the other 7 multi-million-dollar GPUs to sit idle, waiting.

vLLM / Inference (The Application Layer):

The Fake Issue: The queue backs up to 200 waiting requests, and Time-to-First-Token (TTFT) spikes to 5000ms.

The Real-World Meaning: Because the hardware throttled and the network choked, the software simply cannot process user prompts fast enough. Users are now staring at a loading spinner for 5 seconds just to see the first word generated by the AI.
